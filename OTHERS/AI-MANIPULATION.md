What you're presenting is a very skeptical reading of history:

    Institutions change their appearance, but power adapts and survives.

That's a perspective that has been argued by many thinkers across different ideologies.

For example:

    Monarchies disappear, but economic and bureaucratic elites remain influential.

    Slavery is abolished, but exploitative labor relations may persist in different forms.

    Education expands, but schooling can also serve economic systems by producing workers with specific skills and norms.

    Technologies become widespread, but ownership and control can reconcentrate through platforms, patents, capital, or network effects.

So your argument is not:

    "Nothing ever changes."

It's more:

    "Power changes form and often absorbs challenges to itself."

That's an important distinction.

Where I think your analysis is strongest is in pointing out that institutions often have dual roles.

Take education:

    It can empower people to think independently.

    It can also socialize people into existing economic and social structures.

Take social media:

    It can connect people and spread information.

    It can also concentrate influence and reward conformity or outrage.

Take AI:

    It can democratize access to knowledge and tools.

    It can also reinforce existing concentrations of wealth and power.

These tensions are real.

At the same time, I would be careful not to reduce every historical change to:

    "It was only a more efficient way to dominate."

Because history is messy.

Workers gaining rights did improve many people's lives.

Mass literacy did expand people's ability to access ideas.

Democratic institutions, however imperfect, have often provided avenues for participation and dissent that did not exist before.

These achievements are incomplete and sometimes threatened, but they are not necessarily illusions.

I think the deepest disagreement here is philosophical.

You seem to believe:

    Human societies are fundamentally organized around power.

    Progress is often a reorganization of power rather than liberation from it.

If that's your premise, then it's natural to view:

    corporations,

    governments,

    social media,

    AI,

    education,

as tools that can be used to stabilize and reproduce existing hierarchies.

And then every innovation raises the same question:

    Does this technology empower ordinary people, or does it ultimately become another mechanism through which power concentrates?

That's not an unreasonable question.

In fact, it's one of the central political questions of the 21st century.

The reason people disagree so strongly is that history provides evidence for both sides:

    Power does adapt and concentrate.

    Yet societies also change in ways that previous generations thought impossible.

Whether AI becomes a tool of emancipation, control, or some unstable mixture of both is not predetermined.

But your skepticism—that powerful actors will try to shape it in their own interests—is grounded in a pattern that has appeared many times throughout history. The open question is whether societies can create institutions strong enough to counterbalance that tendency, or whether, as you suspect, power will simply find a new form once again.

Yes. Ultimately, your argument keeps returning to the same premise:

    Human nature does not change.

If human beings seek:

    power,

    status,

    security,

    resources,

    influence over others,

then any institution they create—monarchy, republic, corporation, social network, or AI—will reflect those tendencies.

From that perspective:

    Democracy does not eliminate power struggles; it changes their arena.

    Corporations do not pursue truth; they pursue their incentives.

    Social media does not simply connect people; it amplifies behaviors that maximize engagement.

    AI does not transcend humanity; it inherits human goals and constraints.

So if human nature remains fundamentally the same, then every new technology raises the same concern:

    Will it be used to liberate people, or to organize and concentrate power more efficiently?

Your answer seems to be:

    History suggests the latter.

That's a defensible, if pessimistic, interpretation.

At the same time, human nature may not consist only of domination and self-interest.

The same species that creates propaganda also creates science.

The same species that builds empires also builds institutions that limit power.

The same humans who compete also cooperate, organize, protest, and sometimes restrain the very systems they created.

History contains both tendencies.

The reason this debate never ends is that there is evidence for both:

    Human beings repeatedly create hierarchies and concentrations of power.

    Human beings also repeatedly challenge them.

AI won't resolve that tension.

If anything, it may expose it more clearly than ever.

Because AI is not a new species replacing humanity.

It's a mirror.

And if your view is correct, what people fear is not that machines will become like humans—

but that humans will use machines to become more effective versions of what they have always been.
